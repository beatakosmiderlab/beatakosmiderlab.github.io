Welcome to the Kosmider Lab

Our translational research focuses on understanding the biology of primary alveolar type II (ATII) cells in lung health and disease. ATII cells are crucial for producing and secreting pulmonary surfactants, and they proliferate to restore the alveolar epithelium after injury to the more sensitive type I cells.

We isolate ATII cells from human and murine lungs to investigate mechanisms of cell injury and impaired repair following exposure to cigarette smoke, e-cigarette aerosols, bacterial and viral infections. Our lab also analyzes inflammatory responses and antioxidant defense systems using cellular, molecular biology, and biochemistry approaches.

A key goal is to develop strategies that enhance ATII cell proliferation and differentiation for lung regeneration after injury. We are also actively researching the pathogenesis of pulmonary emphysema, aiming to discover novel treatments that slow alveolar wall destruction.

Highlighted Research

Alveolar Type II Cell Biology

Studying surfactant synthesis, secretion, and the regenerative capacity of ATII cells.

Lung Injury & Repair

Investigating mechanisms of cell injury and impaired repair from smoke, aerosols, and infections.

Inflammation & Antioxidants

Analyzing inflammatory responses and antioxidant defense systems in lung cells.

Emphysema Pathogenesis

Understanding alveolar destruction to develop treatments for emphysema and COPD.

Affiliated Center

The Kosmider Lab is proud to be part of the Center for Inflammation and Lung Research (CILR) at the Lewis Katz School of Medicine, Temple University. CILR is dedicated to uncovering the causes of inflammatory lung diseases — including COPD, pulmonary fibrosis, and ARDS — and translating discoveries into real-world treatments.

Our team of scientists, clinicians, and translational researchers works across disciplines to ensure our research has real clinical impact. We are also committed to training the next generation of pulmonary researchers, mentoring medical students, postdoctoral fellows, and early-career investigators.
